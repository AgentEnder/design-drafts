# Brief: Threadline (incident timeline tool)

> Generated by `design-drafts:brief` on 2026-05-08. Edits welcome — keep the section headings stable so consuming skills can parse it.
>
> This is a sample brief for a fictional product, included with the skill to show the deterministic output shape. Do not treat any of the claims here as real.

## Audience

- **Who:** Backend engineers on small platform teams (3–10 engineers) who get paged for production incidents and have to reconstruct what happened across services after the fact.
- **Currently using:** A mix of Datadog dashboards, Slack scrollback, and a shared Notion runbook page that nobody updates. Most reconstruction happens in a Slack huddle the next morning.
- **What they care about:**
  - Seeing the full timeline of an incident across services in one place, without losing the conversation that happened during it.
  - Linking from a log line directly to the deploy or config change that preceded it.
  - Not having to copy-paste UTC timestamps between five tabs.

## Intent

- **Primary action:** Start a free-tier workspace and pipe their first incident into it.
- **Primary outcome:** Within the first incident postmortem, the team uses Threadline's shared timeline instead of a Slack scrollback dump. If that happens, retention is essentially solved.
- **Secondary action:** Read the architecture page — the audience is technical and will not start a workspace before they understand how data is collected and stored.

## Constraints

- **Palette:** Near-black background (`#0B0B0E`), warm off-white text (`#F2EFEA`), single accent in muted amber (`#D88A3A`). No gradients. Status colors only on inline incident severity badges.
- **Typography:** IBM Plex Mono for timestamps, log lines, and any monospace UI; Söhne (or Inter as fallback) for prose. Avoid Geist; avoid the Tailwind default stack.
- **Layout shape:** Three-pane: incident list left, timeline center, detail/conversation right. The center pane is the product; the page should feel like the timeline is the page, not a section of it.
- **Density:** Dense — closer to a code editor than a marketing page. Reviewers should see the product working with real-looking data above the fold, not a hero illustration.
- **Must include:**
  - A real-looking timeline screenshot (or live demo) above the fold.
  - One sentence stating the price of the team plan (`$12/seat/month`) on the landing page itself, not behind a click.
  - A link to the architecture page within the first viewport.

## Must-not-look-likes

Cited by entry number from `docs/anti-patterns.md`:

- **#1 — The indigo-to-violet hero gradient:** The product is meant to feel like an instrument, not a SaaS pitch deck. No gradients anywhere.
- **#2 — Glassmorphism plus neon glow on dark mode:** This is dark-themed and that anti-pattern is the obvious trap. Flat surfaces only.
- **#3 — Three-column icon-headline-blurb feature grid:** The features are the timeline view. Show it, don't list three benefits next to outline icons.
- **#6 — Centered hero with eyebrow pill, huge headline, two CTAs:** The hero should be the actual UI, not an announcement.
- **#10 — "Empower your team to...":** Backend engineers will close the tab.
- **#13 — Three-word benefit headlines:** "Fast. Reliable. Yours." would be a self-parody for this audience.
- **#17 — Hero→features→testimonial→CTA→footer skeleton:** The structural default. The page should be: timeline screenshot, one-sentence positioning, install/architecture choice, pricing inline, footer. No "How it works" section.
- The phrase "incident management platform" anywhere on the page — that is the category they're trying to step out of.

Defaults the user is consciously embracing (do not flag in review):

- **#9 — Product screenshot in a fake browser chrome:** Acceptable here, because the product *is* a browser app and the chrome is part of the demo's honesty. Do not tilt it; do not add a fake reflection.

## Voice

- **Adjectives:**
  - **Technical** — like Sourcegraph or Tailscale docs, not like a B2B keynote.
  - **Quiet** — short sentences, no exclamation points, no second-person hype.
  - **Specific** — numbers and concrete behaviors instead of adjectives. "Renders 50,000 timeline entries without dropping frames" beats "blazing fast."
- **Off-limits vocabulary:**
  - "Empower," "supercharge," "unlock," "delightful," "magical," "buttery smooth," "modern," "intuitive."
  - "Built for [adjective] teams" of any kind.
  - "It just works."
  - "Reimagine," "rethink," "transform" as verbs about the category.
- **Reference copy:** From the Tailscale homepage: "Tailscale is a programmable network. Every server, container, VM, and human gets a stable IP, on a single private network." Two sentences. Concrete. No mood words. That register.
