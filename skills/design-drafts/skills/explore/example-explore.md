## Premise

A tool for backend engineers on small platform teams to reconstruct production incidents from logs, deploys, and Slack scrollback into a single timeline. The draft is showing the timeline view itself, not a marketing pitch — the question is whether we can make a reviewer believe "I would actually use this on a Tuesday morning at 9am after being paged."

## Axes under consideration

For each axis discussed: candidate concepts the user reacted to. Concepts marked `[picked]` should carry into the brief.

### theme

- **Quiet Instrument** [picked] — looks like a code editor for postmortems; near-black background, monospace for timestamps and log lines, one warm accent (amber) used sparingly for severity badges.
  - keywords: editorial, restrained, dense, instrument-like
  - pushes against: #1 (indigo→violet hero gradient), #2 (glassmorphism + neon glow on dark mode)
- **Studio Notebook** — paper-warm off-white, a single accent in deep teal, type-driven hierarchy with a serif display face for incident titles.
  - keywords: print-magazine, calm, bookish
  - pushes against: #5 (Inter/Geist defaults), #1 (the gradient)
- **Operations Console** — high-contrast on white, blue-grey palette closer to PagerDuty/Datadog, treats the timeline as a control surface rather than a document.
  - keywords: ops, dashboard, utilitarian
  - pushes against: #2 (no fake-glow), #11 ("built for modern teams" framing)

### layout

- **Three-Pane Workbench** [picked] — incident list on the left, timeline center, detail/conversation right. The center pane is the product; the page should feel like the timeline IS the page, not a section of it.
  - keywords: editor-like, persistent panes, no scroll-stack
  - pushes against: #6 (centered hero with eyebrow pill), #17 (hero→features→testimonial skeleton)
- **Single-Document Scroll** — full-width scrolling timeline, no panes; navigation collapses into a sticky top breadcrumb. Closer to a Notion doc than an IDE.
  - keywords: editorial, single-flow, document-shaped
  - pushes against: #6, #3 (three-column feature grid)
- **Split-Hero with Live Demo** — top half is a one-sentence positioning + animated timeline demo, bottom half is a deeper static timeline. Marketing-y but technically honest.
  - keywords: hybrid, animated, demo-forward
  - pushes against: #17 (reorders the skeleton), but flirts with #6 enough to be risky

### voice

- **Quiet & Specific** [picked] — short sentences, numbers over adjectives ("renders 50,000 timeline entries without dropping frames" beats "blazing fast"), no second-person hype.
  - keywords: tailscale-docs, sourcegraph-docs, instructional
  - pushes against: #10 ("Empower your team to..."), #13 (three-word benefit headlines), #14 ("delightful")
- **Operator Direct** — second-person, terse, slightly grim about incidents. "You got paged at 3am. Here is the timeline."
  - keywords: oncall-dark-humor, terse, trauma-aware
  - pushes against: #11 ("built for modern teams"), but the "you" framing is itself a default — would need to handle carefully
- **Editorial / Long-form** — paragraph-shaped, willing to use a 30-word sentence, treats the landing page like a Stratechery essay about the problem space.
  - keywords: long-form, considered, opinionated
  - pushes against: #13, #15 ("we believe..."), but density risks losing reviewers who skim

## Notes captured

- "I want a reviewer to feel like the timeline is the page, not a section of the page." — recurring all session.
- "No fake gradients anywhere. If we're going dark, it's flat." — said three times.
- "The price has to be on the landing page itself, not behind a click. $12/seat/month." — committed enough to put in the brief, but flagged as needing sales confirmation.
- "I keep saying 'modern' and I should stop. What I mean is 'feels like an instrument I'd already trust.'"
